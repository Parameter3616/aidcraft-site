How to Calculate Your True Freelancer Hourly Rate

If you charge $75 an hour, you probably think you make $75 an hour. You almost certainly don't.

Your quoted rate and your real hourly rate are two different numbers. The gap between them is where freelancers lose money without realizing it. Some clients eat up hours in revision cycles, scope creep, and unpaid admin time that never appears on an invoice. Others are clean and profitable.

Until you calculate your true hourly rate per client, you're guessing which is which.

Why Your Quoted Rate Is Misleading

Your quoted rate only counts the hours you bill. But freelance work includes plenty of time that doesn't show up on invoices: responding to emails, preparing proposals, chasing payments, sitting on calls that run long, and doing revisions outside the original scope.

A client who pays $75/hour but generates 15 hours of unbilled work per month has a very different real rate than one who pays $60/hour with zero overhead.

Step 1: Track All Hours, Not Just Billable Ones

For each client, start logging every hour you spend on their work. This includes the obvious (design, development, writing) and the invisible (email threads, status calls, file organization, revision rounds, invoicing).

Do this for one month. You only need a rough log — even rounding to the nearest 15 minutes works. The goal is to capture the full picture, not achieve stopwatch precision.

Step 2: Calculate Total Revenue per Client

Add up everything a client has paid you in the same period. Include all invoices, retainer payments, and any one-time project fees. If a payment is still outstanding, count only what's actually been received.

Step 3: Divide to Find Your Effective Hourly Rate

Effective Hourly Rate = Total Revenue ÷ Total Hours (billable + unbillable)

This is your real number. Run it for every active client.

Example:

Client A pays $6,000/month. You bill 60 hours but spend 80 total (including calls, emails, revisions).

Effective rate: $6,000 ÷ 80 = $75/hour

Client B pays $3,600/month. You bill 40 hours and spend 42 total.

Effective rate: $3,600 ÷ 42 = $85.71/hour

Client B is more profitable despite paying less overall.

Step 4: Rank and Act

Once you have effective hourly rates for all your clients, sort them from highest to lowest. This ranking tells you exactly where your time is well spent and where it's being wasted.

With this data, you can make informed decisions:

  • Raise rates on low-profitability clients (you now have the numbers to justify it)
  • Set boundaries on unbilled work (cap revision rounds, limit meeting time)
  • Fire unprofitable clients and replace them with work that pays your real target rate
  • Negotiate smarter on new projects because you know your actual floor

Know Your Minimum Rate

There's one more number worth calculating: your minimum acceptable rate. This is the hourly rate below which you're losing money after accounting for taxes, insurance, software, equipment, and your target take-home pay.

The formula works backward from your annual expenses:

Minimum Rate = (Target Income + Annual Expenses + Taxes) ÷ Billable Hours per Year

If any client's effective rate falls below this number, you're subsidizing their work with your own income.
