DIY Small Business Bookkeeping: Schedule C Made Simple

If you're a freelancer, sole proprietor, or side-hustle owner filing a Schedule C, bookkeeping doesn't need to be complicated. You don't need QuickBooks. You don't need an accountant for the basics. You just need a system that tracks the right numbers in the right categories.

This guide walks through exactly what the IRS expects, what to track, and how to build a bookkeeping habit that takes about 15 minutes a week.

What Schedule C Actually Is

Schedule C is the IRS form where sole proprietors report business income and expenses. It attaches to your personal Form 1040. The math is straightforward: gross income minus deductible expenses equals your taxable business profit. That profit gets hit with both income tax and self-employment tax (15.3%).

The lower your taxable profit (legally), the less you pay. That's why tracking every deductible expense matters. A $500 expense you forgot to log costs you roughly $150 in taxes you didn't need to pay.

The IRS Expense Categories You Need to Know

Schedule C has about 20 line items for expenses. Most small businesses only use 8 to 10 of them regularly. Here are the ones that matter most:

CategoryWhat Goes Here
AdvertisingAds, website hosting, domain names, business cards
Contract LaborFreelancers or subcontractors you paid (1099 recipients)
Office ExpensesSoftware subscriptions, printer ink, paper, desk supplies
SuppliesMaterials consumed in producing your product or service
UtilitiesInternet, phone (business portion)
Car & TruckMileage or actual expenses for business driving
TravelFlights, hotels, meals on business trips (meals at 50%)
Home OfficeSimplified method: $5/sq ft up to 300 sq ft ($1,500 max)
InsuranceBusiness liability, professional E&O coverage
Other ExpensesAnything deductible that doesn't fit neatly above

The key rule: an expense must be ordinary (common in your industry) and necessary (helpful for your business). If it meets both criteria and you have a receipt, it belongs on your Schedule C.

Step 1: Separate Your Money

Open a dedicated business checking account. This is the single biggest thing you can do for clean bookkeeping. When all business income goes in and all business expenses go out of one account, you have a built-in audit trail. Most online banks offer free business accounts with no minimum balance.

If you use a credit card for business expenses, get a separate one for that too. Mixing personal and business spending on the same card is how deductions get missed.

Step 2: Record Every Transaction

Once a week, log your income and expenses. For each transaction, capture four things: the date, amount, IRS category, and a short description. That's it. You're not writing an essay. "Mar 3 / $49 / Office Expenses / Notion annual subscription" is all you need.

A simple spreadsheet works perfectly for this. The columns are Date, Description, Category, Income, and Expense. One row per transaction. Sort by date. At any point you can filter by category to see where your money went.

Step 3: Estimate Quarterly Taxes

The IRS expects self-employed people to pay taxes four times a year, not once. The due dates are April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15. If you owe more than $1,000 at year end, you could face a penalty for not paying quarterly.

The rough formula for estimating each payment:

Quarterly Payment = (Net Profit × 0.153) + (Net Profit × Your Income Tax Rate) ÷ 4

For most sole proprietors, setting aside 25-30% of every payment you receive covers both self-employment tax and income tax. Transfer that percentage into a separate savings account the same day revenue hits. It's not your money to spend.

Step 4: Generate a Profit & Loss Statement

At the end of each month, total your income and expenses by category. Subtract total expenses from total income. That's your monthly profit (or loss). This is the same number that eventually flows onto your Schedule C, just broken into monthly chunks so you can spot trends early.

Example — March P&L:

Revenue: $8,200

Expenses: Contract labor ($2,000) + Office ($350) + Advertising ($200) + Car ($180) = $2,730

Net Profit: $5,470

Estimated tax set-aside (28%): $1,531.60

Common Mistakes to Avoid

After talking to dozens of freelancers and sole proprietors, the same problems come up over and over. Most of them are easy to fix once you know they exist.

  • Forgetting to log small recurring expenses. That $12/month app subscription is $144/year in missed deductions.
  • Not tracking mileage. The 2025 IRS rate is 70 cents per mile. If you drive 5,000 business miles, that's $3,500 in deductions. Keep a mileage log or use an app.
  • Waiting until December. Doing a year's worth of bookkeeping in one sitting means you will miss things. Weekly is painless. Yearly is a nightmare.
  • Mixing personal and business. Buy groceries with your business card once, and now you have to explain it. Keep things clean from day one.

When to Upgrade from DIY

DIY bookkeeping works well up to roughly $75,000-$100,000 in annual revenue. Beyond that, or if you have employees, inventory, or multiple revenue streams, it's worth considering dedicated software or a bookkeeper. But for most solo businesses, a well-organized spreadsheet and 15 minutes a week is all it takes.
