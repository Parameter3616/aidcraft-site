S-Corp Salary Optimization: How to Pay Yourself and Maximize QBI

If you run an S-Corp (or you're thinking about electing S-Corp status), the single biggest tax lever you control is how much you pay yourself in salary versus distributions. Get the split right and you save thousands in self-employment tax while unlocking the full 20% Qualified Business Income deduction. Get it wrong and you're either overpaying the IRS or inviting an audit.

This guide breaks down the mechanics, the math, and the strategy — no jargon, no fluff.

Why S-Corp Salary Matters

As a sole proprietor, every dollar of net profit gets hit with self-employment tax at 15.3% (Social Security + Medicare). When you elect S-Corp status, only your W-2 salary is subject to payroll taxes. The remaining profit passes through as distributions, which are not subject to self-employment tax.

The catch: the IRS requires your salary to be "reasonable compensation" for the work you do. You can't pay yourself $10,000 on $300,000 in profit and call the rest distributions. But you also shouldn't pay yourself more than necessary, because every extra dollar of salary costs you 15.3% in payroll taxes that distributions would avoid.

The Reasonable Compensation Standard

The IRS doesn't give a specific formula. Instead, they look at factors like your role, industry, experience, hours worked, and what similar positions pay in your market. Think of it this way: if you had to hire someone to do your job, what would you pay them?

Common benchmarks for sole-owner S-Corps:

Net Profit RangeTypical Salary RangeNotes
$50K – $80K60–70% of profitLower profits mean salary is most of it
$80K – $150K50–60% of profitSweet spot for meaningful tax savings
$150K – $300K40–50% of profitLarger distribution gap, bigger savings
$300K+35–45% of profitSocial Security wage base caps help here

These are guidelines, not rules. The right number depends on your specific situation, but this gives you a reasonable starting range.

The QBI Deduction Connection

Section 199A lets S-Corp owners deduct up to 20% of Qualified Business Income from their taxable income. QBI is essentially your S-Corp profit minus your salary. The deduction is powerful — on $100,000 in QBI, that's a $20,000 reduction in taxable income.

Here's where it gets strategic: your salary reduces QBI. Every dollar you shift from distributions to salary shrinks your QBI deduction. So the optimization is a balancing act:

Lower Salary → Less Payroll Tax + Larger QBI Deduction
Higher Salary → More Payroll Tax + Smaller QBI Deduction

For income above certain thresholds ($191,950 single / $383,900 married filing jointly in 2025), the QBI deduction phases out for specified service trades. Below those thresholds, the full 20% applies regardless of industry.

Running the Numbers: A Real Example

Let's say your S-Corp generates $200,000 in net profit and you're single. Compare two salary scenarios:

Scenario A — $120,000 Salary:

Payroll tax (employer + employee): $120,000 × 15.3% = $18,360

QBI: $200,000 − $120,000 = $80,000

QBI deduction (20%): $16,000

Net tax cost of salary choice: $18,360 − $16,000 × marginal rate

Scenario B — $85,000 Salary:

Payroll tax (employer + employee): $85,000 × 15.3% = $13,005

QBI: $200,000 − $85,000 = $115,000

QBI deduction (20%): $23,000

Net tax cost of salary choice: $13,005 − $23,000 × marginal rate

Scenario B saves $5,355 in payroll taxes and adds $7,000 more in QBI deduction. At a 22% marginal rate, that extra QBI deduction saves another $1,540 in income tax. Total savings from optimizing the split: roughly $6,900 per year.

Multiply that over 5 or 10 years and you can see why this matters.

The Social Security Wage Base

There's a ceiling on Social Security tax. In 2025, only the first $176,100 of wages is subject to the 12.4% Social Security portion. Above that, you only pay the 2.9% Medicare tax (plus 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax above $200K for single filers).

This means for high-earning S-Corps, the payroll tax savings from lower salary are primarily on the Social Security portion. Once your salary exceeds the wage base, additional salary only costs 2.9-3.8% in payroll taxes — which changes the math on optimal splits at higher income levels.

Common Mistakes

  • Setting salary too low. The IRS has won multiple court cases against S-Corp owners paying unreasonably low salaries. If your role would command $80K in the market and you're paying yourself $30K, that's a red flag.
  • Ignoring QBI entirely. Many S-Corp owners optimize for payroll tax savings without factoring in the QBI deduction. The two interact — you need to model both together.
  • Not adjusting annually. Your optimal salary changes as your profit changes. A salary that was perfect at $100K profit might be too high or too low at $200K. Revisit the split every year.
  • Forgetting state taxes. Some states tax S-Corp distributions differently than salary. Your optimal federal split might not be optimal after state taxes.

When S-Corp Election Makes Sense

The S-Corp structure starts saving money when your net self-employment income consistently exceeds roughly $50,000-$60,000 per year. Below that, the cost of running payroll, filing an S-Corp return (Form 1120-S), and the added complexity often outweigh the tax savings. Above that threshold, the savings typically grow with income.

You'll also need to run payroll (even if it's just for yourself), file quarterly payroll tax returns, and file a separate corporate tax return. Budget $500-$1,500/year for payroll service and tax prep if you outsource those.
