Short-Term Rental Tax Deductions: What STR Hosts Can Write Off

Running an Airbnb or VRBO property isn't just a side hustle — it's a business with real tax implications and real deductions. Most STR hosts leave money on the table because they don't know what they can write off or how the IRS classifies rental income. This guide covers every major deduction category, the key tax rules that apply to short-term rentals, and how to track everything so tax season is painless.

How the IRS Classifies Your STR Income

Before diving into deductions, you need to understand how the IRS sees your rental activity. The classification determines which deductions you get and where the income goes on your return.

If the average guest stay is 7 days or less, your STR is treated as an active business, not passive rental activity. This is reported on Schedule C (not Schedule E), and self-employment tax applies. The upside: you can deduct business expenses more aggressively and aren't limited by passive loss rules.

If the average stay exceeds 7 days, income generally goes on Schedule E as rental income. Losses may be limited by passive activity rules unless you qualify as a real estate professional.

Most Airbnb and VRBO hosts with nightly or weekend bookings fall into the Schedule C category.

Depreciation: The Biggest Deduction Most Hosts Miss

Depreciation is a non-cash deduction that lets you write off the cost of your property over time. For residential rental property, the IRS allows straight-line depreciation over 27.5 years. You only depreciate the building value, not the land.

Annual Depreciation = (Purchase Price − Land Value) ÷ 27.5

Example:

Purchase price: $300,000

Land value (from tax assessment): $60,000

Depreciable basis: $300,000 − $60,000 = $240,000

Annual depreciation: $240,000 ÷ 27.5 = $8,727/year

That's nearly $8,700 per year in deductions without spending a dime. Over the life of the property, depreciation often represents the single largest tax benefit for STR owners. Furnishings and equipment can be depreciated faster — typically 5 to 7 years — or expensed immediately under Section 179.

The Personal Use Rule

If you also use the property personally (vacations, family stays), you need to allocate expenses between rental and personal use. The IRS looks at two numbers: rental days and personal use days.

If personal use exceeds the greater of 14 days or 10% of rental days, the property is considered a "personal residence" and your deductions are limited to the amount of rental income — you can't create a tax loss. Days spent doing maintenance don't count as personal use, but you need to document the work you did.

The cleanest approach: keep personal use to a minimum and document every visit with a clear business purpose.

Tracking Your Expenses

The IRS expects you to have records backing up every deduction you claim. For STR owners, that means keeping receipts, bank statements, and a log that shows each expense, its category, and how it relates to the rental.

Best practices that save headaches at tax time:

  • Dedicated bank account and credit card. Separate your STR expenses from personal spending so there's a clean paper trail.
  • Photograph receipts immediately. Paper fades. A phone photo stored in a cloud folder organized by month takes seconds.
  • Log mileage. Every trip to the property for cleaning, maintenance, or guest issues is deductible at the IRS standard rate (70 cents/mile in 2025).
  • Track occupancy and personal use days. Your booking calendar is your primary evidence. Export it at year-end.

Common STR Tax Mistakes

  • Not claiming depreciation. Even if you don't claim it, the IRS treats you as if you did when you sell. Take the deduction now — you'll be taxed on the recapture either way.
  • Missing startup costs. Expenses incurred before your first booking (furnishing, photography, platform setup fees) are deductible. They don't disappear just because you hadn't earned income yet.
  • Ignoring cost segregation. A cost segregation study can reclassify parts of your property into shorter depreciation schedules (5, 7, or 15 years instead of 27.5). On a $300K property, this can accelerate $30,000-$60,000 in first-year deductions.
  • Forgetting state and local taxes. Many cities and counties have occupancy taxes or STR permit fees. These are deductible, but only if you track them.
